SynChrono Module

In many autonomous vehicle simulations, you don't care what happens if two cars collide - it's precisely the situation that you're trying to avoid. In SynChrono we take advantage of that fact and split a simulation of many Chrono::Vehicles across multiple MPI ranks. This let us scale from running ~1 vehicles in real-time to running ~100 vehicles in real-time. The code is in the Chrono repository, and a longer description is on our lab website.

Multibody Dynamics in the rA Formulation

A problem analogous to mapping the two-dimensional surface of the Earth to a flat piece of paper shows up in multi-body dynamics, only in three dimensions. Imagine you're walking the surface of the Earth while someone tells you where to go with cardinal directions (North, East, ...). At some point however you arrive at the North Pole. Now they can no longer tell you to go North, West or East, the only valid direction is South! Where they could previously tell you to move freely along two axes (North-South and East-West), now they just have one.

In multi-body dynamics, the analogue to "position on the globe" is the particular rotation of a three-dimensional body (is your coffee cup upside down, tilted...). One way to represent this is with Euler Angles (think yaw, pitch and roll), but you'll run into the problem above - there will always be states where you can't get the system to change state in a particular way. The physical version of this problem - gimbal lock is very really and actually impacted the Apollo missions.

Besides working very carefully with euler angles, another solution to the problem is to add a fourth variable to describe your system (astronaut Mike Collins jokingly asked mission control for a “fourth gimbal for Christmas”). Rather than four angles, a smart way of adding a fourth variable is by using unit quaternions. These have the added advantage of having much simpler partial derivative expressions than euler angles and so are in many cases preferred.

Yet another solution (the rA formulation for now) is to work directly "on the globe" (integrate over a Lie space) rather than do any of the above conversions "onto a paper map" (to a linear vector space).
